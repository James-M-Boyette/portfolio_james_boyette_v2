The Basics

What is Mad Libs ?
    Mad Libs is a phrasal template word game (created by Leonard Stern and Roger Price). During play, ramatical elements of a story are identified and chosen for replacement:
  • if, say, the original story was about Albert Einstein's discovery of Physics' Special Relativity, a player could choose to alter it so that we learned of his discovery of McDonalds' Special Secret Sauce.
The Goal ?
    One or more players take turns suggesting and substituting-in nouns, verbs, adjectives, and adverbs for elements of the original story.
    ( This game is particularly useful for reinforcing essential grammar, reading comprehension, and vocabulary skills )
The Challenge ?
    Much more of a "party" game, Mad Libs has two essential challenges:
  1. use one's pre-existing grammatical knowledge and inferential logic to supply categorically-correct words, and
  2. be creative. Given that a literal win condition is nearly non-existant, players are encouraged to be as clever as they can. Novel refrences, ironic replacements, "inside-jokes," and even taboo subjects are all ways both an individual and the group can both "win" and score 'bonus points' in the game.
    Note: often, in order to increase the challenge, are neither shown the story, nor told where their word-subsitutions will go. Instead, the gramatical categories are declared on their own, player inputs are recorded, and then the entirety of the story is read-aloud, to the surprise and delight of the group~

Conceptual Challenge

Properly-Scoped ...
    The central challenge of this app wasn't "figure out how to make a Mad Libs app" - it was to create "literally any app" that would be
    -
    1. interesting,
    2. challenging, and yet
    3. not involve objects . . .
    -
    . . . because the cirriculum hadn't-yet introduced the concept. Up to this point, students had only been exposed to
    -
      integers, strings, concatenation, variables, arrays, 'while' loops, and 'for' loops.
    -
    They had not, however, learned about 'key:value' pairs, 'DOM' manipulation, or even 'forEach' loops. My working assumption was that students should be treated as if this project was being given to them within the first two weeks of a bootcamp.
    -
    Problem: How were students going to be able to insert the user data into the story?
    -
    My instinct was to store the user's entries in an object, or use loops to insert them directly into the story's string. Habit and familiarity turned out to be surprising stumbling blocks, because the inability to use the hash objects and nested loops I'd grown so accustomed-to really hung me up for a while. Eventually, I realized that if the user's inputs could be stored individually, that list could be run-through and used to populate the templated story. Mad Libs was going to be an interesting use-case for a "beginner's" substitution algorithm. It would allow students to
    • Store multiple user entries in an array,
    • Store a target string (the story), and then
    • Use both concatenation and a loop to replace specific words with the user data,

Development Challenge # 1

Expanded Options & the UI
    One main challenge I've encountered during development of this app, post-launch, has been
    -
    - how to expand the app's abilities, and then
    -
    - how to design the web page's UI so that those options are intuitive.
'Pre-Populated' Inefficiency
    Originally, the app required the user to go through 28 pop-up questions (because the 'alert' element was a quick and simple way to give students a basic UI for their app). But it seemed obvious that someone (including me) might want to just click a button and see the result. Thankfully, during its original creation, I made a pre-filled list of user answers to expidite the itterative-testing of the algorithm.
    -
    ... I just didn't make it until half-way through process. I know that eventually, I'll get super fast and savvy at this coding thing, but right now, I tend to still 'brute-force' problems more than I feel good about ... After re-running this code file numerous times to work out the bugs, it occured to me, "Hey - maybe speed-clicking the alert 28^ nth times isn't strictly necessary." Then the light dawned and I made the second, "developer" array of user submissions. [face-palm]
    -
    The added logic and array were re-incorporated to produce the "Pre-Populate" action you currently see.

Development Challenge # 2

'Original' Tunnel-Vision
    Are all development stories good ones? What about dumb-in-the-face embarrassing ones? Well, here'goes:
    -
    The next feature to add was a templated-version of the story - one with lots of "fill in the blank" spaces so that people unfamiliar with the game could see what the game looked like at the beginning. This was easy-enough - copy/paste the concatenated story-string a third time and replace ${} with _.
    -
    Then it occured to me: "some people might want to see the actual, original, complete story." I went looking for it, but could only find a back-up copy stored in an early version of the app. I copy-pasted it, and then ran the app ... only to discover that I wasn't getting the pretty, HTML-infused version I'd been serving before. Instead, it was one giant block of text with "//" where the 'p' tags should've been. After 30 minutes of frustration, it occured to me that this wasn't a case of bad JavaScript logic, or bad
    -
    My bootcamp instructor required us to take breaks on the hour, every hour. Up until now, I've scoffed at that. "Power-through!!" I've said - but now, I'm thinking tunnel vision and exhaustion might be causing more problems than I thought. Anyway, this is what led to the 'Original' and 'Reset' actions you now see in the app.
